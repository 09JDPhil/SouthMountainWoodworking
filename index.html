Built by Hand

I grew up watching my father run his own construction company. Long before I picked up a chisel, he taught me what it actually costs to build something well: the early mornings, the risk of betting on yourself, and the satisfaction of standing behind something you made with your own hands.

Later, I spent years at a company whose name has become part of American life, built entirely around getting the details right. What I took from that was a standard: a great experience is never an accident. It is engineered, piece by piece, until it feels effortless. I learned to obsess over the parts no one consciously notices, because those are the parts people feel.

South Mountain Woodworking Company is what happens when those two educations meet. From my father, the belief that ownership means doing the work honestly and standing behind it. From the years after, the instinct to build something that does not just function, but delights.Every piece I build with traditional, visible joinery holds both standards at once: honest work, and work that exceeds what anyone expected walking in.

Built near the mountain

South Mountain Woodworking Company started where it stands. I moved to this area to be closer to family, and the workshop followed not long after. There was never a plan to build a furniture company somewhere else and bring it here. The place came first.

That order matters more than it might sound like it should. Everything we make is built on the same instinct that brought us here: stay close to what matters, and let go of everything else. In the workshop, that means furniture with no unnecessary ornament, no rushed joinery, nothing added just to be seen.

We build with traditional techniques, mortise and tenon, dovetail, joinery left visible rather than hidden beneath the surface, because a piece should show you how it was made and prove to you why it will last.

A well built piece outlives whoever made it. That is the standard we hold every piece to. Not furniture for a season or a trend, but furniture meant to be chosen once and lived with for a generation, the kind of piece someone eventually hands down instead of replaces.

We are named for the mountain because it is a fitting model. Old, unhurried, and unmoved by whatever is loud at the moment. We would like our work to hold up the same way.
